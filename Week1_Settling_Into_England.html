Welcome to England Life

A short note from Teacher Benny, how this programme works, and a few details to personalise it for you.

A note from Teacher Benny

If you're reading this, your English is probably already stronger than you give it credit for. What's often missing isn't grammar or vocabulary — it's confidence: the nerve to start a conversation with a stranger, to ask a question in a shop without rehearsing it five times first, to laugh at a joke you only half understood.

England Life is built around that gap. Over twelve weeks, we'll work on the real situations you'll meet living here — registering with a doctor, small talk at the school gate, a phone call that goes slightly wrong, a joke that doesn't translate. Grammar will support you along the way, but it is never the main event. Communication is.

Go at your own pace. Some weeks you'll fly through; others you'll want to sit with for longer. Both are fine. This is your programme.

Settling Into England

Your first weeks in a new country are a mix of small victories and small frustrations — a letter you finally understand, a form you finally finish, a neighbour who finally remembers your name. This week is about the everyday language of settling in: registering for things, making sense of British paperwork, and getting through those slightly overwhelming first conversations.

Five words you'll meet again and again in your first weeks here.

settle inphrasal verb
To become comfortable in a new place, job, or routine.
"It took Marek about a month to settle in after moving to Leeds."
paperworknoun
The official documents and forms you need to complete or provide, especially for government offices, banks, or landlords.
"There's always more paperwork than you expect when you open a bank account in the UK."
registerverb
To put your name on an official list so a service has your details — for example, with a doctor or for council tax.
"You should register with a GP as soon as you move to a new area."
councilnoun
The local government body responsible for services in your area, such as bin collection, schools, and council tax.
"I called the council to ask about bin collection days."
overwhelmedadjective
Feeling like you have too many things to deal with at once, often causing stress.
"Between the new job, the flat, and the paperwork, she felt completely overwhelmed in her first week."

Five everyday phrases you'll hear before you ever open a textbook.

"Mustn't grumble"
Meaning: A typically understated British way of saying things are okay, even if not perfect — used instead of complaining.
Example: "How's the new flat?" "Oh, mustn't grumble — the boiler's a bit temperamental, but it's home."
Usage note: Said with a slight smile; it signals resilience rather than a real complaint.
"Sorry, after you"
Meaning: A polite phrase used when letting someone go first — through a door, in a queue, into a lift.
Example: Two people reach a doorway together: "Sorry, after you." "Oh — thank you."
Usage note: "Sorry" here isn't an apology — it's a softener, used constantly in British politeness.
"I'll pop to the shop"
Meaning: To go somewhere quickly and briefly.
Example: "I'll pop to the shop for some milk, won't be long."
Usage note: "Pop" suggests something short and casual — very common in everyday speech.
"It's not rocket science"
Meaning: Something isn't difficult to understand or do.
Example: "Filling in this form isn't rocket science — just take your time."
Usage note: Can sound a little impatient if said about someone else's struggle — use it gently.
"Fancy a cuppa?"
Meaning: An invitation to have a cup of tea, often as a way of offering hospitality or a break.
Example: A new neighbour knocks: "Fancy a cuppa before you finish unpacking?"
Usage note: Accepting a "cuppa" is often a small but meaningful step toward being welcomed by someone.
Two Letters and a Cup of Tea
An England Life reading text

Diego had been in Sheffield for eleven days when the second letter arrived. The first had been from the council, welcoming him to the area and asking him to register for council tax within twenty-one days. He had read it three times before he understood what was actually being asked of him. The second letter looked almost identical — same window envelope, same slightly threatening typeface — but this one was from his bank, asking him to confirm his address. He left both letters on the kitchen table, next to a half-unpacked box of kitchen utensils, and felt, for the first time since landing at Manchester Airport, completely overwhelmed.

It wasn't that any single task was difficult. Registering with a GP had taken him fifteen minutes online. Opening a bank account had taken an afternoon, mostly spent waiting. But the tasks kept arriving in pairs and triples, each one slightly different from what he'd expected, each one written in a tone of voice he couldn't quite place — formal, but oddly friendly; serious, but with the occasional joke buried in a sentence about bin collection days. Back home, paperwork had a certain rhythm he understood instinctively. Here, even the rhythm felt foreign.

He was still staring at the letters when there was a knock at the door. His neighbour, a woman in her sixties named Pam, stood on the step holding a tin of biscuits.

"You're the new one in number four, aren't you? Settling in alright?"

"Mustn't grumble," Diego said, trying out the phrase he'd heard a colleague use that morning. It felt strange in his mouth, like borrowed clothes.

Pam laughed properly at that, which surprised him. "Very good. You'll fit right in." She glanced past him at the kitchen table. "Council tax form, is it? Don't worry about that today — go and register tomorrow, it'll still be there. Fancy a cuppa first?"

He hesitated, the way he always did when an invitation arrived faster than he expected, and then said yes. Twenty minutes later, sitting in his half-furnished living room with a mug he didn't recognise and a biscuit he'd never tasted, Diego found himself talking — properly talking, not just answering questions — about his old flat, the weather, the strange politeness of a man at the post office who had said "sorry, after you" to him twice in one queue.

"That's just how it works here," Pam said. "Everyone's terribly sorry about everything, and nobody's actually apologising for anything." She tapped the council letter on the table. "Same with that, really. Looks worse than it is. Most things do."

By the time she left, the letters hadn't moved, but something else had shifted slightly. Diego registered for council tax online the next morning — it took six minutes — and replied to the bank by lunchtime. Neither task had become easier. He had simply stopped facing them alone.

Present Simple & Third Person Singular

We use the present simple for facts, routines, and things that are generally true — exactly the kind of sentences you need to describe your new life. With he / she / it, the verb takes an extra ending.

SubjectVerb formExample
I / you / we / theybase formI register online.
he / she / it+ -s / -esShe registers online.

Spelling rules for he/she/it:

— Most verbs: add -s → live → lives, work → works

— Verbs ending -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, -o: add -es → watch → watches, go → goes

— Verbs ending consonant + y: change y → i, add -es → worry → worries. Irregular: have → has.

Practice: complete the sentence
1. The council the bins every Tuesday. (collect)
2. My neighbour always for the postman. (watch)
3. She an appointment with the GP tomorrow. (have)
4. He about his English on the phone. (worry)

Discussion: the postcard test

Imagine writing a postcard home about your first week in England. You only have room for three details. Say them out loud (to a partner, a tutor, or even your phone). What three details would you choose, and why those three?

Roleplay: at the GP reception

Practise registering as a new patient with a partner. Swap roles afterwards.

Role A — New patientYou've just moved to the area. Explain that you'd like to register, give your name, address, and date of birth, and ask what happens next.
Role B — ReceptionistGreet the patient, ask for their details, explain they need to complete a form, and tell them roughly how long registration takes.

Partner activity: the settling-in interview

Take turns interviewing each other for two minutes each. Interviewer's questions: What's been the easiest part of settling in? What's been the hardest? What's one thing you'd tell someone arriving next week?

Real-life simulation: say it fast

One of the first hurdles in England is saying your own address and date of birth quickly and clearly — on the phone, at a reception desk, in a queue. Practise saying both out loud, three times, at a natural conversational speed, until they stop feeling like a performance.

A few cultural notes that help first encounters go more smoothly.

🚶

Queueing

Queues form almost automatically in England, even where there's no rope or sign. Joining the back of one — and never pushing in — is taken seriously, far more than the politeness of any words you say.

💬

"How are you?" as greeting

Often it's not a real question. The expected reply is short and positive — "Fine, thanks, you?" — even on an average day. Save the honest, detailed answer for someone who already knows you well.

🌦

Weather small talk

Commenting on the weather is a safe, almost universal way to start a short conversation with a stranger — a shop assistant, a neighbour, someone waiting for a bus.

🚪

Neighbour culture

Not every neighbour will knock with biscuits, but a polite nod, a quick "morning," or remembering bin day are all small ways of signalling that you're a good neighbour.
